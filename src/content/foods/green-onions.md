## Are Green Onions Safe for Dogs?

No. Green onions — also called scallions or spring onions — are toxic to dogs. They are literally young onion plants, members of the same *Allium* family as [onions](/onions/), [garlic](/garlic/), [chives](/chives/), and [leeks](/leeks/), and they carry the same hazard.

As with the rest of the family, this is an accurate "toxic" label rather than an overstated one — the allium group is a genuine, well-documented blood toxin, unlike foods whose reputations the evidence doesn't support ([oregano](/oregano/), [lemon](/lemon/), [avocado](/avocado/)).

## The Nuance Most Content Misses: Milder Per Gram, but Eaten in Larger Amounts

This is the point worth getting right about green onions, because it cuts both ways.

**Green onions are somewhat milder in allium concentration per gram than a mature bulb onion.** Read casually, that sounds like they're "less dangerous."

**But dose is amount × concentration — and green onions are usually eaten raw and in larger quantities.** They're not a background ingredient softened by cooking; they're piled fresh onto tacos, stirred through salads, scattered over stir-fries, ramen, noodles, and dips as a generous garnish. A dog that cleans up a plate of nachos or fried rice heavy with raw scallion can take in **more total allium than the "milder per gram" framing suggests** — the larger quantity offsets the lower concentration.

So the honest calibration is the reverse of the usual reassurance: don't downgrade green onions in your mind just because they're mild and leafy. Realistic exposure can be **higher** than for a stronger onion that's used more sparingly.

## The Allium Mechanism, in Brief

Green onions contain the same organosulfur compounds as bulb onions. They oxidise a dog's **red blood cells**, forming **Heinz bodies** and causing **haemolytic anaemia**, with the serious signs **delayed, typically over 3–5 days.** Merck places the clinical threshold for onion toxicosis at roughly **15–30 g/kg of body weight** — and because green onions are eaten in volume, that threshold is easier to approach than it looks. The full dose tables, mechanism, and breed-sensitivity notes are on the [onions](/onions/) hub page.

## Symptoms to Watch For

**Early (hours) — gastrointestinal:** vomiting, diarrhoea, drooling, abdominal pain, reduced appetite.

**Later (typically 3–5 days) — the anaemia:** lethargy, weakness, pale gums, rapid heart rate, rapid breathing, **reddish or brown urine**, collapse in severe cases.

The delayed onset is the trap — a normal-looking dog the next day is expected, not reassuring.

## What To Do

- **A dog that ate a garnish-heavy dish** (scallion-topped tacos, fried rice, ramen) — note the amount and the dog's size and **call your vet or the [Pet Poison Helpline](https://www.petpoisonhelpline.com/poison/onion/)**; the larger raw quantities are exactly why this matters.
- **Small dogs:** lower threshold to call — dose-per-kilogram is the whole issue.
- **A tiny incidental amount** in a healthy large dog: lower concern, but note it and watch for 3–5 days.
- **Don't wait for symptoms** after a significant exposure, and **don't induce vomiting** unless a vet directs you to.

## Frequently Asked Questions

> This FAQ summarises general veterinary toxicology guidance. It's informational only and not a substitute for advice from your own vet.

### Are green onions less toxic than regular onions?

Slightly milder *per gram* — but that's misleading on its own, because green onions are usually eaten raw and in larger amounts. The realistic exposure can be as high or higher. Treat them as the same hazard.

### My dog ate scallions off a plate of leftovers — what now?

Estimate how much and factor in your dog's size, then call your vet. Green onions on takeaway dishes are often abundant, and many of those dishes also contain onion or garlic in the cooking.

### Are the green tops safer than the white part?

No meaningful safety difference for your purposes — both are allium and both are toxic. Don't rely on one part being "the safe bit."
