## Is Tea Safe for Dogs?

The honest answer is that it depends on the type and the amount — and that dependence is the whole story, so we'll lead with it.

**An update worth stating plainly:** this page previously treated tea as flatly **Toxic**. On review, that overstates it. The accurate status is **Caution**, because the thing that makes tea a concern — **caffeine** — varies enormously between types, and a meaningful share of what people call "tea" (herbal teas) contains **no caffeine at all**. A generic "tea is toxic" warning would misrepresent a category that runs from a genuine stimulant dose down to literally caffeine-free. (This is the same calibration discipline we apply throughout the site — matching the label to what the evidence says, here dialling a warning *down* rather than up.)

Caffeine is still a real toxin for dogs, and this page takes it seriously. But the dose is the whole question.

## How Much Caffeine? It Depends Entirely on the Type

Approximate caffeine per cup of brewed tea:

| Tea type | Caffeine (per cup, approx.) |
|---|---|
| Black tea | ~40–50 mg |
| Green tea | ~30–50 mg |
| White tea | ~15–20 mg |
| **Herbal tea (chamomile, peppermint, rooibos, hibiscus)** | **~0 — usually not true "tea" at all** |

That herbal-tea row is the practical headline: most herbal "teas" are infusions of caffeine-free plants and carry none of the caffeine concern. A few exceptions — yerba maté, or blends with added green tea or cacao — do contain caffeine, so a label glance is worth it.

For context, brewed tea sits well below [coffee](/coffee/) — which is why coffee remains a firmer "keep away entirely" while tea is dose-dependent.

## The Higher-Risk Scenario: Eating Dry Tea Bags or Leaves

The most important caveat on this page: **the risk from tea is highest when a dog eats the dry tea bags or loose leaves — not when it laps a bit of brewed tea.**

Dry leaves are **concentrated caffeine**, undiluted by a mug of water, so a dog that chews through a box of tea bags can take in far more caffeine than one that stole a sip from your cup. On top of that, the **tea bag itself** (paper, string, staple, or the plastic mesh of some pyramid bags) is a minor foreign-body and GI-obstruction risk. A dog that's eaten tea bags — especially a small dog, or several bags — warrants a call to your vet.

## Why Caffeine Matters

Caffeine is a **methylxanthine**, the same chemical family as the theobromine in [chocolate](/chocolate/). Dogs are more sensitive to it than people and clear it more slowly — the Merck Veterinary Manual gives caffeine a **half-life of about 4.5 hours** in dogs. It's a heart-and-nervous-system stimulant: at a high enough dose it causes agitation, a racing heart, tremors, and in severe cases seizures.

We're deliberately **not** printing a precise "milligrams per kilogram" cutoff, because the confirmed veterinary sources don't publish a clean one for caffeine, and a false-precision table would be worse than an honest range. The workable rule: **a lap of brewed tea is a small dose; concern scales up with the amount, the caffeine level of the specific tea, and how small the dog is** — and eaten dry leaves are the scenario to act on.

## Symptoms to Watch For

Caffeine effects typically begin within **1–2 hours** and can last **12–36 hours** (VCA):
- Restlessness, hyperactivity, pacing, or vocalising
- Racing or irregular heart rate
- Vomiting
- Increased urination
- Tremors, and in severe cases seizures

## What To Do

- **A lap of brewed tea, healthy adult dog:** low concern. Note the type (black/green vs. herbal) and keep an eye out for restlessness.
- **A dog that ate dry tea bags or loose leaves:** call your vet or a poison line — this is the concentrated-dose scenario, plus a bag-obstruction angle. More urgent for small dogs or multiple bags.
- **A small dog, or a large amount of caffeinated tea:** call your vet; watch for the stimulant signs above over the next several hours.
- **Caffeine-free herbal tea:** generally not a caffeine concern — but confirm it's genuinely herbal and doesn't contain xylitol or other additives if it's a sweetened blend.

## Frequently Asked Questions

> This FAQ summarises general veterinary toxicology guidance. It's informational only and not a substitute for advice from your own vet, who knows your dog.

### Is tea toxic to dogs?

It's dose-dependent, which is why we call it Caution rather than Toxic. The caffeine in black and green tea is a real concern in quantity, but a lap of brewed tea is a small dose, and most herbal teas are caffeine-free.

### Can dogs drink herbal tea?

Most herbal teas (chamomile, peppermint, rooibos, hibiscus) are caffeine-free and aren't a caffeine concern. Confirm it's truly herbal (some blends add green tea or maté), serve it plain and cool, and skip any with xylitol or lots of additives. It's still not something a dog needs.

### My dog ate a tea bag — should I worry?

This is the scenario to take more seriously than spilled brewed tea: dry leaves are concentrated caffeine, and the bag is a minor obstruction risk. Note how many and your dog's size, and call your vet, especially for a small dog.

### Is green tea safer than black tea for dogs?

Slightly lower caffeine on average, but the difference is modest and both are dose-dependent. Neither is a beverage to share; white tea is lower still, and herbal is the only genuinely caffeine-free group.
