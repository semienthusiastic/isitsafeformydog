## Is Cocoa Powder Safe for Dogs?

No — cocoa powder is **the most concentrated common chocolate hazard in a kitchen.** It's essentially pure cocoa solids, which is where the theobromine lives, so gram for gram it's far more dangerous than any chocolate bar. The mechanism is the standard chocolate toxicity covered on the [chocolate](/chocolate/) hub; what matters here is the concentration.

## The Most Concentrated Common Form

Cocoa powder carries about **28.5 mg/g of theobromine — roughly 807 mg per ounce, or ~150 mg per tablespoon** (Merck). That's around **12 times the theobromine of a milk chocolate bar** by weight, and more than [dark chocolate](/chocolate/) or baking chocolate.

Because it's so concentrated, **small amounts reach a toxic dose**:
- A **10 kg (22 lb) dog** reaches mild-toxicity signs (~200 mg theobromine) from **a bit over a tablespoon.**
- A **5 kg (11 lb) dog** gets there from **around two teaspoons.**

And it's a **fine powder** — easy for a dog to lick up from a spill, a dusted countertop, or an open tub, and easy to underestimate by volume. Unsweetened and "baking" cocoa are the most concentrated; see [hot chocolate mix](/hot-chocolate-mix/) for the heavily-diluted drinking version, which is a very different dose. The [chocolate](/chocolate/) hub has the full weight-by-weight table.

## Where Dogs Encounter Cocoa Powder

- **Baking** — a spilled tub, a dusting on the counter, a licked bowl or whisk
- **Recipes and mixes** — brownie batter, frosting, chocolate sauces
- **Cocoa mulch** — garden mulch made from cocoa bean shells is high in theobromine and smells appealing to dogs; a genuine outdoor exposure worth knowing about

## Symptoms & What To Do

Signs begin **within 1–4 hours** (not delayed for days like [allium](/onions/) poisoning): vomiting, diarrhoea, restlessness, a racing heart; at higher doses tremors, seizures, and heart-rhythm changes.

Given how little cocoa powder it takes, **treat any real ingestion as urgent** — call your vet or a poison line with your dog's **weight** and the **amount** (in teaspoons/tablespoons if you can estimate). Don't wait for symptoms. Full dose math on the [chocolate](/chocolate/) hub.

## Frequently Asked Questions

> This FAQ summarises general veterinary toxicology guidance. It's informational only and not a substitute for advice from your own vet.

### Is cocoa powder more dangerous than a chocolate bar?

Much more, by weight — it's the most concentrated common form, around 12× a milk chocolate bar gram for gram. A couple of teaspoons can matter for a small dog, whereas the same dog would need to eat far more of a milk chocolate bar.

### My dog licked up some spilled cocoa powder — should I worry?

Yes — because it's so concentrated, even a small licked-up amount can be a real dose, especially for a small dog. Estimate the amount, note your dog's weight, and call your vet or a poison line.

### Is baking cocoa the same as hot chocolate mix?

No — and the difference is large. Baking/unsweetened cocoa powder is highly concentrated; [hot chocolate mix](/hot-chocolate-mix/) is cocoa heavily diluted with sugar and milk powder, which puts it at roughly milk-chocolate level per gram. Don't treat the two as equivalent.
