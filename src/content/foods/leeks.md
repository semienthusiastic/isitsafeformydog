## Are Leeks Safe for Dogs?

No. Leeks are toxic to dogs. They're members of the *Allium* family alongside [onions](/onions/), [garlic](/garlic/), [chives](/chives/), and [green onions](/green-onions/), and the toxicology is identical to the rest.

As across the whole allium family, this is an accurate "toxic" label — a genuine, well-documented blood toxin — not one of the overstated reputations the site pushes back on ([oregano](/oregano/), [lemon](/lemon/), [avocado](/avocado/)).

## Lower Incidence, Not Lower Toxicity

This is the framing to hold firmly, because it's easy to get backwards.

Dogs are poisoned by leeks **less often** than by onions or garlic. But the reason is about **exposure, not potency**: leeks are usually eaten **cooked into soups, stews, quiches, and prepared dishes**, rather than handed over as raw snacks, dropped as table scraps, or baked into obviously dog-tempting foods like garlic bread. Fewer everyday situations put a leek in a dog's mouth.

That is **not** the same as leeks being safer. **Gram for gram, leeks are as toxic as any other allium.** A whole leek is a substantial mass of allium, and a serving of leek-and-potato soup or a leek-heavy casserole is a real exposure. Cooking doesn't reduce the toxicity — it just happens to be how leeks usually reach a dog. So "less commonly a problem" should never quietly become "less dangerous."

## The Allium Mechanism, in Brief

Leeks contain the same organosulfur compounds as the rest of the family. They oxidise a dog's **red blood cells**, forming **Heinz bodies** and causing **haemolytic anaemia**, with the serious signs **delayed, typically over 3–5 days.** The full dose maths, mechanism detail, and breed-sensitivity notes are on the [onions](/onions/) and [garlic](/garlic/) hub pages; the same clinical thresholds apply here.

## Symptoms to Watch For

**Early (hours) — gastrointestinal:** vomiting, diarrhoea, drooling, abdominal pain, reduced appetite.

**Later (typically 3–5 days) — the anaemia:** lethargy, weakness, pale gums, rapid heart rate, rapid breathing, **reddish or brown urine**, collapse in severe cases.

A dog that seems fine the day after a bowl of leek soup is not necessarily clear — the anaemia is the delayed part.

## What To Do

- **A dog that ate a leek-based dish** (leek-and-potato soup, a leek casserole, a quiche): note the amount and the dog's size and **call your vet** or an animal poison line. Leek-heavy servings are a genuine allium dose despite being cooked.
- **Small dogs:** lower threshold to call.
- **A tiny incidental amount** in a healthy large dog: lower concern, but note it and watch for 3–5 days.
- **Don't wait for symptoms** after a significant exposure, and **don't induce vomiting** unless a vet directs you to.

## Frequently Asked Questions

> This FAQ summarises general veterinary toxicology guidance. It's informational only and not a substitute for advice from your own vet.

### If dogs rarely get poisoned by leeks, are they low-risk?

Low-*incidence* isn't the same as low-risk. Leeks are just less often put in front of dogs. Gram for gram they're as toxic as onions and garlic, so a leek-heavy meal is a real exposure.

### Does cooking leeks in soup make them safe?

No. Cooking doesn't remove the toxic compounds — and soups and stews are the most common way leeks actually reach a dog.

### My dog ate leek-and-potato soup — should I call the vet?

Yes, if it was more than a trivial lick — estimate the amount, factor in your dog's size, and call. Watch for the delayed signs over 3–5 days.
