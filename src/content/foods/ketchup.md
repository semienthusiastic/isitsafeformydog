## Is Ketchup Safe for Dogs?

Ketchup is not acutely dangerous for a dog that licked a smear off the plate. It is also not food to give deliberately, and there is no version of regular ketchup that is appropriate as a condiment for dogs. The concerns rank clearly:

1. **Onion powder** — present in standard ketchup; small per tablespoon, cumulative over time
2. **Sugar and sodium** — minor at typical quantities; worth knowing for dogs on restricted diets
3. **Xylitol** — genuinely serious if present; not documented in mainstream products, but a label-check on any reduced-sugar variant is worthwhile

**UK readers:** In British English, "tomato sauce" typically means ketchup. This is that page. For plain pureed tomato sauce (the canned cooking base), see [tomato sauce](/tomato-sauce/).

## Onion Powder — the Primary Concern

Standard ketchup — Heinz and most supermarket equivalents — contains onion powder as a standard flavouring ingredient. Onion is an allium toxin: it causes oxidative damage to red blood cells leading to Heinz body formation and haemolytic anaemia (Merck Veterinary Manual). All forms retain full toxicity — raw, cooked, dehydrated, and powdered. Cooking does not neutralise the hazard.

**Dose context per tablespoon:**

A 10 lb (4.5 kg) dog has an acute onion threshold of roughly 68–135 g of fresh onion (Merck: 15–30 g/kg body weight). A tablespoon of ketchup contains a very small fraction of onion powder — well under 1 g of onion equivalent. A dog that laps up a tablespoon of ketchup is nowhere near acute toxicity territory.

The realistic concern with ketchup is not a single exposure but **cumulative habit**: a dog given ketchup on food regularly, or that cleans the ketchup-smeared plates as a routine, accumulates a small allium load with each exposure. Allium toxicity from repeated small doses is documented — the ASPCA lists garlic and onion as causing "red blood cell damage leading to anaemia" without a lower dose threshold below which cumulative exposure is categorically safe.

A one-off lick: low concern. Ketchup as a regular addition to meals: stop it.

For the full garlic and onion picture — mechanism, acute thresholds, symptoms, what to do — see the [garlic](/garlic/) and [onions](/onions/) pages.

## Sugar and Sodium — Minor at Typical Quantities

Standard ketchup contains approximately 4 g of sugar and 160 mg of sodium per tablespoon, based on typical product labelling across major brands. Neither figure creates an acute problem from occasional small exposure in a healthy dog.

**Sugar:** A tablespoon is a negligible amount relative to any dose at which [sugar](/sugar/) would cause meaningful harm. The concern with sugar in a dog's diet is long-term — contributing to dental issues, weight gain, and blood glucose dysregulation — not acute toxicity from a tablespoon.

**Sodium:** 160 mg per tablespoon is modest. For context, a dog would need to ingest far more than any realistic ketchup quantity to approach a sodium concern (see [salt](/salt/) for thresholds). For dogs on a vet-prescribed low-sodium diet due to cardiac or renal disease, it is worth noting.

**Vinegar** is a standard ketchup ingredient — the source of its acidity. Vinegar in the quantities found in ketchup is not a toxicological concern for dogs; see [vinegar](/vinegar/).

## Xylitol — Label-Check on Reduced-Sugar Variants

[Xylitol](/xylitol/) is genuinely dangerous to dogs. The Merck Veterinary Manual documents a hypoglycaemia threshold of approximately 100 mg/kg body weight, with liver necrosis possible above 500 mg/kg. VCA Hospitals confirms the mechanism: xylitol causes a rapid insulin release that crashes blood glucose within 30 minutes of ingestion. There is no meaningful dose of xylitol from food a dog should eat.

**The xylitol picture for ketchup specifically:** No mainstream ketchup — standard, reduced-sugar, or sugar-free — has been identified in any veterinary source or our own label review as containing xylitol. The ASPCA's list of foods to avoid does not name ketchup as a xylitol source. The Merck and VCA entries on xylitol toxicosis list gum, candy, mints, peanut butter, baked goods, dental products, and medications — not ketchup. Reduced-sugar ketchup products typically use sucralose as the sweetener, not xylitol.

PetMD's general note that ketchup "sometimes contains xylitol" is precautionary hedging not backed by specific product identification. We haven't found a product it applies to.

**That said:** Xylitol can appear in any reduced-sugar food product, and the consequences if it does are serious enough that checking the ingredient list of any reduced-sugar or sugar-free ketchup before your dog encounters it is a reasonable precaution. On a standard ketchup — the dominant category — it is not a concern in practice.

## Reading the Label

For standard ketchup, the ingredient list will typically include: tomato concentrate, [vinegar](/vinegar/), [sugar](/sugar/), [salt](/salt/), onion powder, and spices. That onion powder entry is the concern — it confirms the allium content.

For reduced-sugar or sugar-free ketchup: check the sweetener. Sucralose is not a concern. Xylitol would be — though no mainstream product reviewed here uses it.

"Spices" and "natural flavoring" can technically include allium derivatives in small amounts. This is more reliably a concern in products like commercial broth than in ketchup, where onion powder is already named explicitly.

## Symptoms to Watch For

**From onion powder in large quantities or cumulative exposure:**
- Onset typically 3–5 days after significant allium ingestion — dog may appear normal initially
- Lethargy, weakness, reduced appetite
- Pale, white, or yellowish gums
- Rapid heart rate, laboured breathing
- Dark or reddish-brown urine

**From xylitol (if present — check label of sugar-free variants):**
- Rapid onset within 30 minutes: weakness, vomiting, loss of coordination
- Collapse, seizures in severe cases
- If xylitol is confirmed on the label and your dog ate any amount: this is an emergency; call your vet immediately

For typical ketchup exposure — a lick off the floor, a smear from a plate — neither of the above is a realistic outcome.

## What To Do

**Dog licked ketchup off the floor or plate:** Low concern. No vet call needed for a healthy adult dog at a small quantity. Note that it happened.

**Dog ate a significant amount of standard ketchup:** The onion powder load from a meaningful quantity is worth noting. Monitor for allium symptoms over the following week. A vet call is warranted if your dog is small (under 10 lb), a puppy, or already anaemic.

**Dog ate reduced-sugar or sugar-free ketchup:** Check the ingredient list for xylitol. If xylitol appears: call your vet immediately — do not wait. If the sweetener is sucralose or another non-xylitol sweetener: the picture is the same as for standard ketchup.

**Ketchup as a regular habit:** Stop it. Cumulative allium exposure from a daily small dose is a real concern even when no individual serving seems significant.

## Frequently Asked Questions

> This FAQ summarises general veterinary guidance. It's informational only and not a substitute for advice from your own vet, who knows your dog.

### My dog licked ketchup off a burger bun — is that a problem?

A small amount of ketchup on a shared bite of food is unlikely to cause harm in a healthy adult dog. The onion powder content per tablespoon is well below acute toxicity thresholds, and a lick-scale exposure is even less. Note it happened; don't make it a habit.

### Why does ketchup contain onion powder if it's a tomato product?

Onion powder is a standard flavouring in ketchup recipes — it adds savoury depth to the tomato base. Most major brands include it, including standard household names. It's listed by name in the ingredient list, making it straightforward to identify.

### Is there a dog-safe ketchup?

Some pet-specific condiment products are marketed as dog-safe alternatives. These would need to be specifically formulated without alliums — any "ketchup" marketed for dogs should list its ingredients clearly. Standard supermarket ketchup is not an appropriate condiment for dogs.

### Does cooking or diluting ketchup make it safer?

Cooking does not neutralise allium toxicity — the Merck Veterinary Manual is explicit that all forms, including dehydrated and powdered, retain full toxicity. Diluting it in food reduces the per-serving dose but doesn't eliminate the allium content. The cumulative concern remains.

### Can dogs eat [tomatoes](/tomatoes/)?

Ripe tomatoes themselves are not toxic to dogs — the ASPCA and AKC both confirm ripe tomato flesh is non-toxic. The concern with ketchup isn't the tomato; it's the onion powder and the other added ingredients. See the [tomatoes](/tomatoes/) page for the full picture on fresh tomatoes.
